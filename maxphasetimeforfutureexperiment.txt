## 1. Yellow Change Intervals (Fixed)
Georgia DOT (GDOT) and ATLDOT strictly calculate yellow times based on the approach's 85th-percentile speed limit using the standard ITE (Institute of Transportation Engineers) kinematic formula. The posted speed limit on both Peachtree and Piedmont at this junction is 35 MPH. [2, 3] 

* 
* Peachtree Rd NE (Thru & Left Turns): 4.0 seconds
* Piedmont Rd NE (Thru & Left Turns): 4.0 seconds
* Simulation Rule: Yellow times never dynamically change; keep these locked. [4] 
* 

## 2. All-Red Clearance Intervals (Fixed)
This intersection is physically wide, requiring an all-red buffer to clear the internal intersection box before conflicting phases get a green light. [5] 

* 
* All-Red Phase: 2.0 to 2.5 seconds (Use 2.0s for through phases, 2.5s for left-turn phases to account for clearance path length).
* 

## 3. Green Time Parameters (Dynamic/Actuated)
Because the intersection operates on an actuated-coordinated system, the green time is defined by a range rather than a fixed number. Vehicles crossing loop detectors or video cameras "extend" the green light up to a maximum threshold. [6, 7] 

| Parameter | Mainline (Peachtree Rd Thru) | Side Street (Piedmont Rd Thru) | Left-Turn Phases (Protected) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minimum Green ($G_{min}$) | 15.0 seconds | 10.0 seconds | 7.0 seconds |
| Passage Time (Extension) | 2.0 to 3.0 seconds | 2.0 to 2.5 seconds | 2.0 seconds |
| Max Green ($Max\_G$ - Off-Peak) | 60.0 seconds | 45.0 seconds | 20.0 seconds |
| Max Green ($Max\_G$ - Peak) | 110.0 seconds | 75.0 seconds | 35.0 seconds |

## 4. Background Cycle Lengths (Coordination)
If your digital twin simulates grid-level synchronization with adjacent lights (e.g., Peachtree at Maple Dr or Piedmont at Lenox Rd), you must use specific Cycle Lengths to force the intersection to repeat patterns: [8] 

* 
* AM Peak Hour (7:00 - 9:30 AM): 140 to 150-second total cycle length.
* PM Peak Hour (4:00 - 7:00 PM): 150 to 160-second total cycle length (maximum bandwidth allocated to Peachtree Northbound and Piedmont Eastbound/Westbound splits).
* Off-Peak / Night: 90 to 120-second cycle length, or operating entirely in uncoordinated "free-run" actuated mode. [9] 
* 

## 5. Pedestrian Phase Timing
To simulate realistic multi-modal behavior, do not forget pedestrian calls, which override minimum vehicle greens: [10] 

* 
* Pedestrian Walk (White Hand): 7.0 seconds minimum. [11] 
* Flashing Don't Walk (Ped Clearance): 18.0 to 22.0 seconds (calculated based on a 3.5 ft/s walking speed across the 65-75 foot street width).
* Leading Pedestrian Interval (LPI): If simulating modern ATLDOT standards, give pedestrians a 3.0-second head start before the parallel vehicular traffic gets a green bulb.
* 

## Recommended Simulation Setup

   1. Define the phase numbering using standard NEMA TS-2 dual-ring logic (Phases 2 and 6 for Peachtree Thru; Phases 4 and 8 for Piedmont Thru; Phases 1, 3, 5, 7 for protected left turns).
   2. Set your detector types to "Presence" at the stop bar (6x6 ft loops). [12, 13] 
   3. If simulating the severe congestion you noted, program a scooter/pedestrian call every 3 cycles, which forces the controller to meet the minimum pedestrian clearance time, starving the competing vehicular green phases and causing the exact backup you see in real life.

No, a flashing yellow arrow (FYA) does not last for 4 seconds. It does not have a set expiration time because it is a dynamic permissive interval, not a change interval. [1, 2] 
For your digital twin simulation, it is crucial to separate how the controller handles a steady yellow versus a flashing yellow.
## 1. Flashing Yellow Arrow (FYA) Duration

* How it works: The FYA allows vehicles to turn left after yielding to oncoming traffic and pedestrians.
* Duration: It runs dynamically for as long as the parallel through-traffic phase has a green light.
* Simulation Rule: If Phase 2 (Peachtree Thru) has a green light for 60 seconds, the concurrent permissive Left Turn (FYA) will flash for that exact same 60 seconds. It does not time out on its own. [2, 3, 4] 

## 2. The Steady Yellow Arrow (The actual 4s change interval)
When the controller decides it is time to terminate a left turn, it switches to a steady (solid) yellow arrow. This is the change interval that requires a strict duration. [2, 4, 5] 
The duration depends entirely on what phase the signal is transitioning from and into:

* Transition A: Protected Green Arrow $\rightarrow$ Flashing Yellow Arrow
* What happens: The green arrow ends, but the parallel through-traffic still has a green light.
   * Timing: The steady yellow arrow lights up for 4.0 seconds to warn drivers that their "protected" right-of-way is ending, before dropping into the flashing yellow (permissive) state. [2, 6, 7, 8, 9] 
* Transition B: Flashing Yellow Arrow $\rightarrow$ Solid Red Arrow
* What happens: The entire approach is losing its green light to let the cross street (Piedmont) go.
   * Timing: The flashing yellow arrow changes to a steady yellow arrow for 4.0 seconds, followed by the 2.5-second All-Red clearance buffer before the signal turns solid red. [2, 6, 10]
